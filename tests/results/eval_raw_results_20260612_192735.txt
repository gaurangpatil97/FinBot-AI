Actual:   To assess the Operating Cash Flow (OCF) quality for FY25 and FY26, we need to compute the ratio of Operating Cash Flow to Net Profit for each year. A higher OCF-to-Net-Profit ratio (close to or above 1) indicates high-quality profit, meaning the profit is well supported by cash flows from operations.

---

### Step 1: Identify Operating Cash Flow (OCF) and Net Profit for FY25 and FY26

- **Net Profit** (from profit_loss sheet):
  - FY25 Net Profit = 194.57 Cr (Source [9])
  - FY26 Net Profit = 383.99 Cr (Source [10])

- **Operating Cash Flow (OCF)** is not directly given in the provided data. However, we can infer OCF from the balance sheet and profit & loss data by using the indirect method formula:

  \[
  \text{OCF} = \text{Net Profit} + \text{Non-Cash Expenses} + \text{Change in Working Capital}
  \]

  Where:
  - Non-Cash Expenses = Depreciation (from profit_loss)
  - Change in Working Capital = Change in Current Assets (Receivables + Inventory) - Change in Current Liabilities (Other Liabilities + Borrowings) or more simply, the net change in working capital components.

---

### Step 2: Extract Depreciation and Working Capital changes for FY25 and FY26

- **Depreciation:**
  - FY25 Depreciation = 347.02 Cr (Source [9])
  - FY26 Depreciation = 443.85 Cr (Source [10])

- **Working Capital Components:**

  Current Assets relevant for working capital:
  - Receivables
  - Inventory
  - Cash & Bank (usually excluded from working capital)

  Current Liabilities relevant for working capital:
  - Other Liabilities
  - Borrowings (usually long-term, but can include short-term borrowings; since no breakdown is given, we exclude borrowings from working capital calculation to be conservative)

  We will calculate change in working capital as:

  \[
  \Delta WC = (\text{Receivables}_{t} + \text{Inventory}_{t}) - (\text{Receivables}_{t-1} + \text{Inventory}_{t-1})
  \]

  Since liabilities breakdown is not clear, we will consider only net current assets change (Receivables + Inventory) as proxy for working capital change.

- FY24 to FY25:
  - Receivables FY24 = 576.63 Cr (Source [18])
  - Inventory FY24 = 1040.82 Cr (Source [18])
  - Receivables FY25 = 920.59 Cr (Source [19])
  - Inventory FY25 = 1332.05 Cr (Source [19])

  \[
  \Delta WC_{FY25} = (920.59 + 1332.05) - (576.63 + 1040.82) = 2252.64 - 1617.45 = 635.19 \text{ Cr increase}
  \]

- FY25 to FY26:
  - Receivables FY25 = 920.59 Cr (Source [19])
  - Inventory FY25 = 1332.05 Cr (Source [19])
  - Receivables FY26 = 1110.54 Cr (Source [20])
  - Inventory FY26 = 1745.41 Cr (Source [20])

  \[
  \Delta WC_{FY26} = (1110.54 + 1745.41) - (920.59 + 1332.05) = 2855.95 - 2252.64 = 603.31 \text{ Cr increase}
  \]

---

### Step 3: Calculate Operating Cash Flow (OCF)

\[
OCF = Net\ Profit + Depreciation - \Delta WC
\]

Note: Increase in working capital is a use of cash, so it is subtracted.

\[

\[

\[
\text{OCF-to-Net-Profit} = \frac{OCF}{Net\ Profit}

- FY25:

\[
\frac{-93.60}{194.57} = -0.48
\]

- FY26:

\[
\frac{224.53}{383.99} = 0.59
\]

- **FY25:** The OCF-to-Net-Profit ratio is negative (-0.48), indicating that despite reporting a net profit of 194.57 Cr, the company had a net cash outflow from operations. - Profit Before Tax (PBT) in FY2017: ₹36.67 Cr  
- Tax expense in FY2017: -₹43.07 Cr (negative tax)  
- Net Profit in FY2017: ₹79.74 Cr  

The effective tax rate (ETR) is calculated as:  
\[
\text{ETR} = \frac{\text{Tax Expense}}{\text{Profit Before Tax}} \times 100 = \frac{-43.07}{36.67} \times 100 = -117.5\%
\]

This means the company recorded a tax credit (negative tax expense) that is larger than its PBT, resulting in Net Profit exceeding PBT.

---

2. **Mechanics explanation:**

- The negative tax expense of ₹43.07 Cr indicates the company recognized a tax benefit or credit in FY2017. This could arise from several legitimate accounting treatments such as:  
  - Deferred tax asset recognition due to carry-forward losses or timing differences.  
  - Tax incentives, rebates, or refunds received during the year.  
  - Adjustments or reversals of prior year tax provisions.

- Because the tax credit (₹43.07 Cr) exceeds the PBT (₹36.67 Cr), the net profit (PBT - Tax) becomes higher than PBT, i.e., ₹79.74 Cr.

---

3. **Forensic concerns to flag:**

- **Unusual magnitude of tax credit:** A tax credit exceeding the PBT by over 100% is unusual and warrants scrutiny. It may indicate aggressive tax accounting or recognition of deferred tax assets without sufficient evidence of future taxable profits.

- **Potential earnings management:** Inflating net profit by recognizing large tax benefits can mislead stakeholders about the company’s true profitability.

- **Verification of tax asset realizability:** The company should have robust documentation and justification for recognizing such a large tax credit, including future profit projections supporting deferred tax asset recoverability.

- **Audit and disclosure:** The auditor’s Key Audit Matters (KAM) or notes to accounts should discuss this anomaly. Absence of clear disclosure or explanation in the annual report or MD&A would be a red flag.

---

**Calculation steps summary:**

- Extract PBT and Tax from FY2017 profit & loss:  
  PBT = ₹36.67 Cr, Tax = -₹43.07 Cr  
- Calculate ETR:  
  \[
  \frac{-43.07}{36.67} \times 100 = -117.5\%
  \]  
- Compare Net Profit:  
  \[
  \text{Net Profit} = \text{PBT} - \text{Tax} = 36.67 - (-43.07) = 79.74 \text{ Cr}
  \]
